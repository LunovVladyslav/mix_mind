# Reverb Theory & Usage

## Core Principle: Sends, Not Inserts
The golden rule of mixing reverb:
- Use reverb on a SEND/AUX channel, not as an insert on the source
- Send multiple instruments to ONE shared reverb → glues the mix
- Different amounts per instrument via send level (pre-fader)

## When to Use Reverb (Context Rules)

**Use reverb when:**
- Dry recorded instruments need space and depth
- Elements sound disconnected/floating without context
- Mix sounds flat and two-dimensional
- Lead vocals need to sit in a 'room'
- Acoustic instruments (piano, strings, drums) need natural space

**Do NOT use reverb on:**
- Sub bass and 808 (reverb on low frequencies = muddy mess)
- Kick drum main channel (kills punch; room mics are separate)
- Already-wet signals (re-reverbing = washy)
- Mix bus or master (almost never)

## Reverb Types by Use Case
- **Room**: Small, tight, natural. Best for drums, snare, percussion.
- **Plate**: Smooth, bright. Best for vocals, snare, lead instruments.
- **Hall**: Long, lush. Best for orchestral, pads, background elements.
- **Chamber**: Warm, vintage. Best for jazz, acoustic, classic rock.
- **Spring**: Colored, retro. Best for guitar, surf, vintage styles.

## Pre-Delay Rule
Pre-delay separates the dry signal from the reverb tail:
- Short pre-delay (0-15ms): tight, intimate, element sounds 'in' the room
- Medium pre-delay (15-30ms): clear separation, element sounds 'at' the room
- Long pre-delay (30-80ms): wide, dramatic separation
- Sync to BPM: pre-delay in ms = 60000/BPM/16 (one 16th note)

## Reverb Decay by Genre
- EDM/House: 0.8-1.5s (tight, punchy spaces)
- Hip-Hop/Trap: 1.5-3s on snare, short on 808
- Rock: 1-2s room, 2-3s plate on vocals
- Pop: 1.5-2.5s plate (bright and smooth)
- Jazz: 2-4s natural hall/chamber
- Classical/Orchestral: 3-6s large hall
- Cinematic: 4-8s (dramatic, wide)

## High-Pass Filter on Reverb Send
ALWAYS high-pass the reverb return at 200-400Hz:
This prevents low-frequency buildup that muddies the mix.
